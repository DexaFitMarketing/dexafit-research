GE Lunar vs Hologic — Why Your T-Scores Don't Match

The same patient, scanned on two different DEXA platforms, will get meaningfully different T-scores, body fat percentages, and lean mass values. This is not measurement error — it is a systematic, well-documented cross-manufacturer discordance that most clinicians and patients are never told about.

April 2026
DexaFit Research Team
DexaFit Research
Key Finding

GE Lunar and Hologic are the two dominant DEXA manufacturers worldwide, together accounting for the vast majority of clinical and research DXA scanners in operation. Despite measuring the same physical properties — bone mineral density, lean mass, and fat mass — their systems produce systematically different numerical outputs. The discordance is not random noise. It is consistent, directional, and large enough to change clinical diagnoses.

DexaFit's analysis of same-person scans across its GE Lunar network found strong within-platform consistency: over three-quarters of cross-location comparisons agreed within 3 lbs on lean mass, and when narrowed to a 14-day window — where meaningful body composition change is physiologically unlikely — agreement tightened to approximately 1.3 lbs lean mass and under 1 percentage point body fat. The manufacturer boundary is where the numbers break down: crossing from GE to Hologic or back produces approximately 4× more discordance than switching between GE locations. And critically, the direction is not consistent — sometimes GE reads higher lean mass, sometimes lower. Two people scanned back-to-back on the same two machines can show different offsets in different directions.

Why This Happens

The differences between GE and Hologic are not a flaw in either system. They are the result of independent engineering paths that diverged decades ago. Both are precise; they are simply not interchangeable. Four specific sources drive the gap.

  • Different calibration references. Each manufacturer calibrates against proprietary phantoms with different compositions. "Zero" is defined differently on each platform — this is arguably the most fundamental difference, because it is the baseline from which all measurements are derived. There is no universal phantom that bridges the two systems.
  • Different tissue decomposition algorithms. The software that translates X-ray attenuation into "lean mass" and "fat mass" is proprietary to each manufacturer. The trunk region is where this matters most — bone, organs, lean tissue, and fat all overlap in the same anatomical space, and each system uses different mathematical models to separate those overlapping signals. This is why cross-manufacturer discordance tends to be largest in trunk-based measurements.
  • Different reference populations. Bone density T-scores compare a patient to a "young healthy adult" reference. Hologic's reference populations draw directly from NHANES III for certain skeletal sites; GE applies validated conversion equations to that same Hologic-derived NHANES III data to produce GE-equivalent reference values. Whole-body composition references diverged from different starting points entirely. A T-score of −1.5 on a GE Lunar machine and a T-score of −1.5 on a Hologic machine do not describe the same bone density in absolute terms.
  • Different beam geometries. Both GE and Hologic use fan-beam systems, but with different engineering approaches. GE uses a cerium K-edge filter to produce dual-energy X-rays; Hologic uses a switched-pulse technique that alternates between two energy levels. Their fan-beam widths, detector arrays, and approaches to magnification correction, scatter correction, and edge detection all differ — and these differences propagate into measurement output.
⚠ Clinical Risk

A patient diagnosed with osteopenia (T-score between −1.0 and −2.5) on one platform may receive a normal diagnosis (T-score above −1.0) on the other — or vice versa. Studies have documented misclassification rates between manufacturers of up to 30% for individual patients at diagnostic thresholds. This is not theoretical — it happens routinely when patients change clinics, move cities, or receive serial scans at facilities with different scanner brands.

The Compositional Fingerprint

One of the most encouraging findings from DexaFit's cross-location analysis is what might be called the compositional fingerprint — the specific pattern of how lean mass and fat mass distribute across the trunk, arms, and legs. This signature appears to travel across GE systems with very high fidelity.

In DexaFit's data, lean-to-fat ratio showed a correlation of r = 0.98 across GE machines at different locations, and trunk fat distribution held at r = 0.94. In practical terms, the pattern that matters most for clinical interpretation — where a person carries mass and in what proportion — is well-preserved across GE-based locations even when absolute values show small differences. This means that even when a specific number shifts slightly between locations, the clinical story the scan tells remains consistent.

This is a meaningful finding. It suggests that for the purposes of trend tracking, risk stratification, and coaching — where ratios and distributions matter more than precise absolute values — the DexaFit GE network functions as a coherent measurement system. The fingerprint travels. The clinical picture holds.

Why DexaFit's Within-GE Consistency Is Stronger Than Average

The within-GE consistency observed across DexaFit locations is not accidental. Published literature on multi-site DXA studies — which use mixed facilities with variable protocols — reports much higher variability than what DexaFit's network shows. Three operational factors explain the difference.

  • Permanent installation. Every DexaFit scanner is permanently installed, never subjected to transport or road vibration. Transport is one of the most common causes of calibration drift in mobile DEXA units, and eliminating it from the equation removes a significant source of measurement instability.
  • Daily phantom calibration. Every DexaFit location runs a manufacturer-specified calibration check every day the scanner operates. Drift gets caught before it accumulates into systematic error. Most clinical facilities do not calibrate at this frequency.
  • Standardized positioning protocols. Positioning is one of the largest sources of technician-dependent variability in DEXA scanning. Hand placement alone can shift regional results by clinically meaningful amounts. DexaFit's standardized positioning protocols across all locations are what make cross-location comparisons possible — and what distinguishes DexaFit scan data from data collected in uncontrolled clinical or research settings.

The ISCD (International Society for Clinical Densitometry) and major clinical guidelines universally recommend that serial bone density monitoring be performed on the same machine at the same facility — specifically to avoid cross-manufacturer discordance corrupting longitudinal comparisons. A patient who was scanned on a GE Lunar in 2022 and a Hologic in 2024 cannot have their T-scores meaningfully compared without cross-calibration.

Cross-Calibration Formulas

The ISCD and research groups have published cross-calibration equations that allow approximate conversion between manufacturer platforms. These are regression-based estimates derived from studies where the same patients were scanned on both systems. They are not exact — they reduce but do not eliminate discordance — and should be applied cautiously in individual clinical decision-making.

Lumbar Spine BMD (L1–L4)

GE Lunar → Hologic: BMDHologic = (0.906 × BMDLunar) − 0.025
Hologic → GE Lunar: BMDLunar = (1.074 × BMDHologic) + 0.054

Source: Genant et al. (1994), Hui et al. (1997). Validated in multiple independent cohorts. Standard error of estimate approximately 0.01–0.02 g/cm².

Total Hip BMD

GE Lunar → Hologic: BMDHologic = (0.979 × BMDLunar) − 0.031
Hologic → GE Lunar: BMDLunar = (1.008 × BMDHologic) + 0.048

Cross-calibration precision is lower at the hip than the spine. Use these estimates for population-level analysis, not individual clinical diagnosis changes.

For body composition, cross-calibration equations exist but are less precise and show higher residual variance. The Shepherd et al. (2012) equations are most commonly cited, but the error range is large enough that individual body fat percentage comparisons across platforms should be made cautiously and with explicit disclosure to patients.

What This Means for DexaFit Data

DexaFit's clinical network operates primarily on GE Lunar (Prodigy and iDXA) systems. All reference ranges, percentile curves, and body composition data published by DexaFit Research are derived from GE Lunar measurements and should be interpreted within that context. Within the GE ecosystem, DexaFit data shows strong cross-location consistency — over 75% of within-GE cross-location comparisons agree within 3 lbs on lean mass, and the compositional fingerprint (lean-to-fat ratios and regional distribution patterns) transfers with r = 0.98 fidelity. When a user has prior results from a Hologic-based scan, direct numerical comparison is not appropriate. The correct approach is a fresh baseline scan on the GE platform, with explicit communication to the user about why their numbers may appear to shift.

The Occult Osteopenia Problem

One of the most clinically significant consequences of cross-manufacturer discordance is what might be called occult osteopenia — patients whose bone density is low enough to warrant monitoring or treatment on one platform, but who appear normal on another. DexaFit internal data analysis suggests this affects a meaningful proportion of patients who present with results from outside facilities.

The practical implication is that a patient who received a "normal" bone density result at their physician's office (Hologic system) and then gets a DexaFit scan (GE Lunar system) may see a higher T-score on the DexaFit report — and could incorrectly conclude their bone health has improved. Conversely, a patient with a GE Lunar scan from a DexaFit location who then gets scanned on a Hologic at their hospital may see a lower T-score and become alarmed without cause.

The solution is consistent documentation of scanner manufacturer and model in all DXA reports, explicit patient education about cross-manufacturer discordance, and a clear recommendation to track bone density on the same platform over time wherever possible.

Practical Guidance — What To Do
  • Within the GE network, cross-location scanning works well. If a user has previously scanned at another GE-based DexaFit location, their trends are directly comparable. The DexaFit data shows within-GE consistency strong enough that trend tracking holds across locations. Reassure them accordingly.
  • Always flag a manufacturer switch before the scan. If a user's previous scan was on a Hologic system, inform them before scanning. Recommended framing: "Your previous scan was on a different manufacturer's system. Both are accurate, but they use different calibration references — so some of your numbers may appear to shift. This is not a change in your body composition. Going forward, your DexaFit scans will be directly comparable to each other."
  • Focus on the fingerprint, not the decimals. When a user is concerned about small differences between scans or locations, redirect their attention to ratios and distribution patterns — lean-to-fat ratio, trunk fat proportion, regional distribution. These carry the strongest signal and travel across GE systems with very high fidelity (r = 0.98). Trends in these metrics are more meaningful than absolute number comparisons.
  • Do not compare body composition percentages across platforms. A 28% body fat on a GE Lunar report and 28% on a Hologic report do not represent the same body. Cross-platform body composition comparison has higher residual error than bone density cross-calibration and should not be presented to patients as equivalent.
  • Encourage consistency in scan timing and preparation. Scanning at the same time of day, with similar hydration and food status, on the same platform reduces all sources of variability simultaneously. The more variables controlled, the more precisely trends become visible.
  • Note the manufacturer and model on every report. A T-score of −1.5 means something different depending on which platform generated it. Without the scanner manufacturer documented, the number is ambiguous and potentially misleading to any subsequent clinician who reads it.

Frequently Asked Questions
My T-score improved between two scans. Could it just be a platform switch?

Yes — if your two scans were performed on different manufacturer systems, the apparent improvement may be entirely or partially an artifact of cross-manufacturer discordance rather than a true change in bone density. Before concluding that your bone health has improved, check whether both scans were performed on the same scanner brand. If they were not, the results are not directly comparable without applying cross-calibration equations.

True bone density changes of the magnitude needed to shift T-score categories (from osteopenic to normal, for example) typically require 1–3 years of treatment with bisphosphonates or other bone-modifying therapies. A sudden large improvement between two scans close in time is a strong signal to check the scanner manufacturer.

Which system is more accurate — GE Lunar or Hologic?

Neither is inherently more accurate than the other in absolute terms. Both systems are validated, calibrated against physical standards, and widely used in clinical research. The discordance between them is a calibration and reference database difference, not a quality difference. Each system is accurate and consistent within its own reference frame — the problem arises only when results from one system are compared to results from the other without cross-calibration.

DexaFit's network operates primarily on GE Lunar (Prodigy) systems. Our reference ranges and percentile data reflect GE Lunar measurements specifically.

My doctor's office uses Hologic. My DexaFit scan is on GE Lunar. Can I compare them?

You can approximate a comparison using published cross-calibration equations (see the formulas above), but the comparison will carry uncertainty that individual clinical decisions should not be based on. For bone density, the ISCD cross-calibration formulas reduce but do not eliminate the discordance, and residual error remains at approximately ±0.01–0.02 g/cm².

For body composition (body fat %, lean mass), cross-platform comparison is even less reliable. The most practical recommendation is to use each scan in its own context — your DexaFit results relative to DexaFit reference ranges, your hospital results relative to their system's reference ranges — rather than comparing absolute numbers directly.

Does the software version matter, or just the manufacturer?

Both matter. Within a single manufacturer, major software version updates (for example, GE Lunar's transition from enCore software versions) can introduce small but measurable shifts in output values — similar in mechanism to cross-manufacturer discordance, but typically smaller in magnitude. ISCD guidelines recommend documenting both scanner manufacturer and software version on all DXA reports, and noting when a facility upgrades their scanner software, so that longitudinal data can be interpreted with appropriate context.
